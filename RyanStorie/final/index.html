Concussions Over Time (2012–2014)

In the past two decades, few issues in sports have drawn as much concern and scientific inquiry as concussions. Once dismissed as “getting your bell rung,” concussions are now recognized as a form of mild traumatic brain injury with serious short- and long-term consequences. From 2012 to 2014, the National Football League alone recorded hundreds of concussions, underscoring the severity and frequency of head injuries at the sport’s highest level. Data from this period shows not only the prevalence of concussions but also the extensive time athletes missed from playing, measured in weeks and total snaps lost.

Yet, the NFL represents just the tip of the iceberg. Beneath it lies a youth and high school sports culture where concussions are often underreported, misunderstood, or minimized, exposing countless young athletes to similar risks without the medical resources professional players receive.

Between 2012 and 2014, concussions led to significant lost playing time across NFL teams. Injured players missed hundreds of weeks collectively, often being sidelined for one or more games. Some injuries resulted in extended absences or even career-ending consequences.

The “Snaps Missed” chart reveals that certain positions, particularly those involving high-speed collisions like wide receivers, running backs, and defensive backs, accounted for a disproportionate share of concussion-related absences. Not only did individual players face downtime, but teams experienced a cumulative toll, with performance and season outcomes affected.

Snaps Missed as a Result of Concussions

While professional athletes receive advanced care and are subject to strict return-to-play protocols, the same cannot always be said for younger athletes. Each year, up to 1.9 million U.S. youth experience sports-related concussions. Football accounts for a substantial portion of these injuries. A study analyzing emergency room visits found that 90 percent of football-related traumatic brain injuries occurred in children aged 5 to 18.

High school athletes are particularly vulnerable, with concussions making up approximately 9 to 13 percent of all athletic injuries. Unlike the NFL, where injury reports are mandatory and closely monitored, concussions at the youth and high school levels are often underreported or even unnoticed. Many young athletes fail to report symptoms because they lack awareness of the seriousness or fear being sidelined and disappointing coaches, teammates, or scouts.

"There is an added risk of enduring another concussion before a concussion is not fully healed."

The implications of repeated concussions can be profound. In recent years, the medical community has gained a clearer understanding of CTE, a degenerative brain disease linked to repeated head trauma. Although much of the early research focused on retired NFL players, studies have since confirmed that even athletes who never reach the professional level are at risk. Research from Boston University found that 41 percent of 152 deceased athletes under the age of 30 showed signs of CTE.

This finding dismantles the notion that only long careers in professional football lead to serious brain disease. In fact, the damage may begin much earlier, during formative years when the brain is still developing.

The NFL has responded to the concussion crisis with several initiatives, including improved helmet technology, changes to game rules to discourage high-risk tackles, and more stringent medical protocols. Independent neurological consultants are now present at games to assess players showing potential signs of head injury. Additionally, in recent years, the use of Guardian Caps has been implemented during practices to reduce impact forces.

While these efforts represent progress, critics argue they are not enough and sometimes serve more as public relations strategies than comprehensive solutions.

At the grassroots level, tackling the concussion crisis is more complex. Many youth and high school programs lack the funding for advanced protective gear or on-site medical professionals. Coaches and parents may not be fully educated about concussion symptoms or the importance of rest and recovery. Even when guidelines are in place, cultural factors can discourage adherence.

"It's pretty well known that returning the kickoff is a really dangerous part of the game where a lot of concussions are sustained."

Football culture traditionally prizes toughness and playing through pain, values that can lead athletes to conceal injuries. Changing this mindset requires more than just rule changes; it demands a shift in how athletes, coaches, and communities perceive injury and health.

Education is a cornerstone of prevention. Programs that inform young athletes, parents, and coaches about the signs and risks of concussions can lead to better reporting and safer play. State laws mandating concussion education and return-to-play protocols have been enacted across the United States, spurred by high-profile tragedies where young athletes died or suffered severe long-term damage after returning to play prematurely.

Yet enforcement and implementation vary widely. Some schools embrace the guidelines enthusiastically, while others lag behind due to lack of resources or administrative support.

Policy changes at the national and state levels can also play a vital role. Standardizing return-to-play protocols and ensuring they are medically sound protects athletes and reduces liability for schools and organizations. Research continues into equipment innovations beyond Guardian Caps, including helmet designs that better absorb and distribute impact forces. Still, no equipment can fully eliminate the risk. As experts note, the only way to completely avoid concussion risk is to avoid activities that involve collisions, which is a reality unlikely to change given football’s deep-rooted popularity.

"As players become more aware and education improves, the goal is to see fewer snaps lost to preventable concussions."

Beyond education and policy, a cultural shift is essential. Athletes need to feel empowered to report symptoms without fear of stigma or benching. Coaches and teams must prioritize player welfare over competitive success. Encouragingly, some signs of this shift are emerging. High-profile NFL players have publicly discussed their concussion experiences, raising awareness and reducing stigma. More parents are becoming proactive in seeking safer environments for their children, and some leagues have introduced non-contact variations of football for younger age groups.

Despite these advances, challenges remain. Underreporting persists at all levels. Even in the NFL, where protocols are stringent, some players have admitted to hiding symptoms to stay on the field. In youth and high school sports, where medical oversight is less consistent, the problem is even more pronounced.

As a result, data like that from 2012 to 2014 may understate the true extent of the concussion crisis. Looking forward, sustained efforts are needed to build on the progress made and address lingering gaps. Research into brain injury continues to evolve, offering hope for better diagnostic tools and treatment options.

Collaboration among medical professionals, sports organizations, educators, and families is essential to create an environment where player health is truly paramount. Concussions may never be completely eliminated from football, but with vigilance, innovation, and a commitment to cultural change, their impact can be reduced. The data from 2012 to 2014 serves as a stark reminder of the human cost of head injuries in football. It also underscores the importance of ongoing vigilance at all levels of the sport. The journey toward safer play is far from over, but with continued effort and awareness, the next generation of athletes can face a future with fewer risks and better protections.
